⭐ PAGE 12 — ASD TESTING PROTOCOLS & MEASUREMENT FRAMEWORK


(Required for SBIR, IRB, NIDILRR, ASD Clinics, and School Pilot Approval)


EchoAuth is evaluated through structured, low-risk, clinically aligned testing across both educational and therapeutic environments. The goal is to validate:

• communication accuracy

• identity confirmation reliability

• user stress reduction

• predictability and consistency of system output

• overall usability for autistic individuals with diverse support needs


All testing follows minimal-risk human subjects criteria and does not involve biometrics, emotional inference, or unsupervised use.


⸻


12.1 Testing Environments


EchoAuth is evaluated in three controlled settings:


1. Clinical ASD Centers

• Speech therapists

• Behavioral specialists

• Occupational therapists

Focus: communication intent mapping, sensory safety, structured interaction flow.


2. Special Education Classrooms

• Students with IEPs

• Paraeducators and teachers

• School psychologists

Focus: daily communication, task initiation, need expression, safety alerts.


3. In-Home Environment (Voluntary)

• Guardian-supervised

• Limited session length

Focus: independent expression, reduction of behavioral escalation triggers.


Testing NEVER occurs without a trained adult present.


⸻


12.2 Participant Criteria


To ensure inclusive yet safe testing:


Inclusion Criteria


Individuals aged 5–22 with:

• ASD Level 1–3

• non-verbal or limited-verbal communication

• sensory sensitivities

• difficulty expressing needs reliably

• existing IEP or therapeutic plan


Exclusion Criteria


EchoAuth cannot be tested with:

• active crisis or behavioral emergencies

• unregulated seizure conditions triggered by screens

• minors without guardian consent

• users unable to tolerate digital interfaces


No Clinical Diagnoses Are Collected


We only collect:

• age range

• communication support level

• guardian-approved notes


No medical records.

No diagnostic data.

No therapy logs.


⸻


12.3 Testing Procedure (Structured Workflow)


Each participant completes 4–6 supervised sessions, each lasting 10–15 minutes.


Step 1 — Orientation (1–2 minutes)

• Guardian/clinician explains the session

• EchoAuth interface shown via calm-mode screen

• No data collection begins until user engages


Step 2 — Familiarization (2 minutes)


User explores:

• need-grid

• preference selector

• calm-mode UI


System observes interaction speed, not behavior quality.


Step 3 — Guided Tasks (5 minutes)


Clinician presents 3–4 simple tasks, such as:

• “Show me what you need right now.”

• “Choose the symbol for how you feel.”

• “Tell me if you want to continue or stop.”


EchoAuth interprets selections through NI-AI structured mapping.

Output must be predictable, non-dynamic, and clinically safe.


Step 4 — Independent Expression (3–5 minutes)


User selects intentions or needs without guidance.

EchoAuth logs:

• communication accuracy

• time-to-selection

• system stability


Step 5 — Session Closure (1 minute)

• Guardian or clinician reviews log

• User exits calm-mode

• Session safely terminates


No data persists beyond retention rules.


⸻


12.4 Measurement Metrics (Quantitative + Qualitative)


EchoAuth is evaluated using 5 primary quantitative metrics and 3 qualitative metrics.


⸻


Quantitative Metrics


1. Communication Accuracy Rate (CAR)


Definition:

% of correct interpretations matched to clinician-verified intent.


Target Phase I: ≥ 30–40% improvement over baseline.


⸻


2. Identity Confirmation Reliability (ICR)


Definition:

EchoAuth’s ability to maintain a stable session identity pattern without biometrics.


Target: ≥ 95% session stability

(no unintended resets; no identity confusion)


⸻


3. Time-to-Expression Reduction (TTER)


Definition:

How quickly a user can express a need versus traditional methods.


Goal: 10–35% reduction in time after 3 sessions.


⸻


4. System Predictability Score (SPS)


Definition:

Consistency of EchoAuth outputs (no unexpected phrasing, tone, or behavior).


Goal: 100% predictable per UMCE constraints.


⸻


5. Sensory Tolerance Score (STS)


Definition:

User’s ability to maintain engagement without signs of overload.


Goal:

≥ 80% of users complete sessions without fatigue indicators.


⸻


Qualitative Metrics


1. Clinician Feedback

• clarity of communication

• reduction in escalation triggers

• ease of use


2. Guardian Feedback

• sense of safety

• reliability of user expression

• transparency and control


3. User Engagement Observations

• calmness

• willingness to interact

• reduced frustration


Collected using standardized ASD observation rubrics.


⸻


12.5 Risk Mitigation & Safety Procedures


EchoAuth testing includes strict safeguards:


1. Real-Time Stop Condition


If at any point user exhibits:

• agitation

• withdrawal

• sensory overload

• refusal gestures


→ Session stops immediately.

No further data collected.


⸻


2. Calm-Mode Auto-Trigger


If user interaction becomes erratic:

• contrast lowers

• interface simplifies

• timing slows

• color palette shifts to neutral


⸻


3. No Behavioral Interpretation


EchoAuth does not analyze emotion, behavior, or mood.

Clinicians interpret user actions — NOT the system.


⸻


4. Guardian Override


At any moment, guardian may:

• pause

• terminate

• delete session

• request data export


⸻


5. No Off-Script Output


EchoAuth may not:

• generate emotional suggestions

• offer advice

• respond conversationally

• infer unvalidated intention


UMCE ensures fail-closed consistency.


⸻


12.6 Reporting & Phase I Deliverables


Each testing cycle produces:


1. Session Reports

• CAR

• ICR

• TTER

• SPS

• STS

• clinician notes


2. Safety Log (Non-Identifiable)

• time of overload

• triggers

• system mode changes


3. Phase I Summary


For SBIR reviewers and ASD clinics:

• aggregate metrics

• safety compliance

• system stability

• participant acceptance
