I. Layered Taxonomy of Delegation Drift Mitigations


(Conceptual, Non-Visual, System-Level)


This taxonomy organizes delegation-drift mitigations by where they operate in the system stack, from surface-level correction to deep structural control.


⸻


Layer 5 — Observational & Forensic Controls


(Highest, weakest control)


Includes:

• Continuous monitoring and alerting

• Audit trails and logging

• Performance metrics and anomaly detection


Role:

Detects and explains drift after it occurs.


Limitation:

Does not prevent the first unsafe action.

Failure must exist before it can be observed.


⸻


Layer 4 — Behavioral Adaptation & Correction


Includes:

• Retraining and fine-tuning

• Adaptive updates / online learning

• Drift metrics and self-correction loops


Role:

Improves model behavior over time.


Limitation:

Treats drift as inevitable.

Assumes authority to act remains intact during correction.


⸻


Layer 3 — Statistical & Structural Robustness


Includes:

• Ensemble methods

• Redundancy and parallel agents

• Cross-validation and routing


Role:

Reduces error probability and single-point failure.


Limitation:

Accuracy improvements do not address authorization.

Correct behavior can still be unauthorized behavior.


⸻


Layer 2 — Procedural Governance & Oversight


Includes:

• Human checkpoints

• Approval workflows

• Role definitions and policy boundaries


Role:

Constrains behavior through process and supervision.


Limitation:

Authority is advisory and timing-dependent.

Humans can be bypassed, delayed, fatigued, or ambiguous.


⸻


Layer 1 — Execution Authority Enforcement (EchoAuth Layer)


(Lowest, strongest control)


Includes (EchoAuth):

• Machine-enforced authority validation

• Presence- and continuity-based authorization

• Explicit refusal as a system state

• Invalidation of cached permissions

• Collapse of agency under ambiguity


Role:

Defines whether action is allowed to exist at all.


Property:

Unsafe execution is structurally impossible, not merely discouraged or corrected.


⸻


Key Insight of the Taxonomy


All commonly cited mitigations for delegation drift operate above the execution layer.

They manage, observe, or correct behavior after authority has already been granted.


EchoAuth operates below them, redefining delegation itself.


Delegation drift is not primarily a monitoring failure.

It is an authority placement failure.


⸻


II. Internal Doctrine — Design Compass (One Paragraph)


EchoAuth Doctrine (Canonical)


EchoAuth is built on the principle that delegation drift arises not from insufficient monitoring or imperfect models, but from allowing systems to act when human authority is unclear, stale, or implicit. Rather than observing or correcting unsafe behavior after it emerges, EchoAuth enforces authorization at the execution layer, making authority machine-enforced and refusal a valid, expected system state. When presence, continuity, or role-based authority cannot be verified, agency collapses rather than redistributing to the system. This design ensures that safety does not depend on vigilance, accuracy, or adaptation, but on structural prevention: actions that should not occur are made impossible by construction.
