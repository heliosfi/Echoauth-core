📄 PAGE 30 — IRB ETHICS OVERVIEW & USER TESTING PROTOCOL


Institutional Review Board Pre-Submission Language (Minimal Risk Study)


This page provides the ethical framework, participant protections, data safeguards, and testing workflow required for EchoAuth’s Phase I user evaluation with autistic and non-verbal individuals.


⸻


I. STUDY CLASSIFICATION


Risk Level: Minimal Risk (As defined by 45 CFR 46.102)


The study does not involve:

• medical procedures

• biological data

• invasive devices

• biometrics

• audio/video recordings

• experimental therapies

• deception

• physical interventions


EchoAuth operates solely as a digital assistive communication platform, supervised by trained staff or caregivers at all times.


⸻


II. PURPOSE OF THE STUDY


The objective of the Phase I IRB protocol is to evaluate:

1. Communication accuracy:

How effectively users express needs and selections through EchoAuth.

2. Identity confirmation reliability:

Whether users can consistently use the EchoAuth identity pathways without stress or confusion.

3. User comfort and sensory compatibility:

Reduction of frustration and sensory overload.

4. Caregiver/clinician workflow improvement:

Whether EchoAuth reduces time and emotional load required to interpret user needs.


No therapeutic claims are made.

This is an assistive technology evaluation, not a medical intervention.


⸻


III. PARTICIPANT POPULATION

• Autistic children, adolescents, and young adults

• Ages 5–25

• Non-verbal, minimally verbal, or verbally inconsistent

• Participants must have:

✔ guardian consent

✔ staff/therapist supervision

✔ stable daily environment


Exclusions (for IRB safety):


Participants will NOT be enrolled if they are:

• in acute psychiatric crisis

• exhibiting self-harm behavior

• unable to tolerate digital displays or structured prompts

• without a caregiver/guardian available for consent


⸻


IV. INFORMED CONSENT PROCESS


1. Guardian Consent


All participants require written informed consent from:

• parents

• legal guardians

• authorized caregivers


Consent documentation includes:

• study purpose

• duration

• description of device use

• confidentiality statement

• withdrawal rights

• risk explanation

• benefits explanation

• contact information for IRB and PI


2. User Assent (When Applicable)


For users who can assent, staff will provide simplified, sensory-safe explanations using:

• icons

• yes/no cards

• short plain-language statements


⸻


V. STUDY PROCEDURES


The evaluation consists of non-invasive digital interactions conducted in a quiet, supervised environment.


Procedure Steps

1. Baseline Session (5–10 minutes)

• user introduced to device interface

• caregiver identifies preferred communication categories

2. Calibration Session (10–20 minutes)

• user explores communication templates

• caregiver adjusts sensory settings

• identity-confirmation workflow introduced

3. Structured Trial Sessions (10–15 minutes each)

• 3–5 tasks presented

• user selects options to express needs or emotions

• staff records:

✔ response clarity

✔ latency

✔ user stress level

✔ sensory interference

4. Debrief

• staff discusses session with caregivers

• system logs are reviewed

• no personal identifiers stored


Total study time per user: 30–60 minutes.


No continuous monitoring. No automated decisions.

Participants may stop at any time.


⸻


VI. RISKS AND MITIGATION STRATEGIES


Potential Risks


The only foreseeable risks are:

• mild frustration if user cannot find an option

• sensory discomfort if brightness or contrast is not adjusted

• fatigue from digital interaction


No physical or emotional harm is reasonably expected.


Risk Mitigation

• sensory settings adjustable in real time

• shortened sessions

• caregiver override option

• option to discontinue immediately

• predictable UI design to reduce overwhelm

• system output always non-judgmental and non-evaluative


⸻


VII. POTENTIAL BENEFITS


While no guaranteed benefit can be claimed, potential improvements include:

• faster expression of needs

• reduced behavioral escalation due to unmet needs

• increased user independence

• reduced caregiver emotional burden

• improved identity verification in unfamiliar environments


These benefits align with the goals of SBIR, NIH, NSF, and NIDILRR programs.


⸻


VIII. CONFIDENTIALITY & DATA PROTECTION


Data Collected


Only the following non-identifying data are stored:

• session response times

• category selections

• number of successful communications

• caregiver observations

• system performance logs


No PII is collected (names, photos, voice, biometrics = prohibited).


Storage

• encrypted device storage (AES-256)

• local or controlled institutional server

• no cloud transmission unless approved


Access

• only authorized staff

• parents may request data deletion at any time


All data handling follows:

✔ FERPA

✔ HIPAA (if clinic setting)

✔ COPPA

✔ ADA digital accessibility requirements


⸻


IX. WITHDRAWAL RIGHTS


Participants may withdraw at any time without penalty.


Pilot Site or HeliosFi may terminate participation for:

• safety concerns

• system misuse

• compliance violations
