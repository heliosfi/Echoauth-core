Human-Safe Authorization


A Structural Approach to Preventing Unauthorized or Premature Action in Government Systems


Nicholas B. Carty


(System Architect — Human-Safe Control Design)


⸻


Executive Summary


Government and institutional systems increasingly rely on automated or AI-assisted processes to support decision-making in environments where errors can produce irreversible consequences. Many historical failures in security, justice, finance, and emergency response do not originate from insufficient intelligence or malicious intent, but from systems acting under ambiguity—before authority, intent, or context are sufficiently clear.


This document presents Human-Safe Authorization, an architectural approach that embeds restraint, pause, and authority separation directly into system design. The objective is not to replace existing procedures or human judgment, but to ensure that systems cannot act outside defined chains of authority, even under time pressure or uncertainty.


⸻


1. The Institutional Risk Landscape


Across government systems, common risk conditions include:

• Distributed authority across multiple roles and agencies

• High-stress operational environments

• Time-sensitive decisions with incomplete information

• Increasing reliance on automated recommendations


In these conditions, procedural safeguards alone are insufficient. Systems that permit execution before authority is unambiguous create exposure to operational failure, legal liability, and public harm.


⸻


2. The Failure Mode: Action Without Authorization Clarity


Institutional failures frequently involve one or more of the following:

• Ambiguous ownership of decision authority

• Automated escalation without explicit human confirmation

• Informal overrides under operational pressure

• Post-hoc attribution of responsibility


These are structural failures, not personnel failures.


Human-Safe Authorization addresses this by enforcing non-bypassable authorization boundaries in system architecture.


⸻


3. Core Design Principle


No system action may occur unless authority, intent, and context are explicitly verified.


This principle is implemented structurally, not procedurally.


Key commitments:

• Systems must be capable of pausing indefinitely under uncertainty

• Authority must be enforced by system design, not operator memory

• Execution must be deterministic and auditable


⸻


4. Pause-on-Uncertainty as an Operational Safeguard


In Human-Safe Authorization, pause is a valid and expected system state.


A pause is triggered when:

• Authority signals conflict or are missing

• Policy conditions are incomplete or inconsistent

• Context deviates from defined parameters


A paused system:

• Does not infer intent

• Does not escalate automatically

• Requires upstream clarification from the appropriate authority


This mirrors safety principles used in physical and nuclear systems, applied to digital and AI-assisted environments.


⸻


5. Authority Separation and Chain of Command


Human-Safe Authorization enforces structural separation of roles, such as:

• Observation / analysis (AI or sensing systems)

• Authorization (human authority holders)

• Execution (system action)


These roles are isolated at the system level:

• No shared credentials

• No implicit escalation paths

• No silent overrides


This preserves chain-of-command integrity and prevents authority drift.


⸻


6. Deterministic Authorization Boundaries


At the authorization boundary:

• Probabilistic outputs are treated as inputs, not decisions

• Authorization outcomes are binary and explicit

• Ambiguity results in pause or denial, not execution


This ensures predictability, reviewability, and legal defensibility.


⸻


7. Audit and Accountability


Every authorization attempt generates an immutable audit record prior to execution, including:

• Time and initiating role

• Requested action

• Policy context

• Authorization outcome

• Reason for grant, denial, or pause


This supports:

• Internal review

• Oversight and compliance

• Post-incident investigation


⸻


8. Applicability Across Government Domains


Human-Safe Authorization is applicable wherever:

• Decisions carry legal, physical, or societal consequences

• Authority is distributed

• Automated systems influence execution


Including but not limited to:

• Defense and national security systems

• Justice and law enforcement workflows

• Financial and benefits authorization systems

• Emergency response and disaster coordination

• Critical infrastructure access control


The approach is domain-agnostic and compatible with existing governance frameworks.


⸻


9. Relationship to AI Use in Government


Human-Safe Authorization does not restrict the use of AI for:

• analysis

• prediction

• decision support


It restricts execution without authorization clarity.


This allows institutions to benefit from AI capabilities while maintaining human accountability and control.


⸻


Conclusion


As automated and AI-assisted systems become embedded in government operations, the primary safety challenge is not intelligence, but restraint. Systems that cannot pause, clarify authority, or prevent premature action introduce unacceptable risk.


Human-Safe Authorization provides a structural approach to ensuring that government systems act only when they are permitted to act—and remain safely inactive when they are not.


⸻


Closing Statement


A system that can act without clear authority is not efficient—it is unsafe.
