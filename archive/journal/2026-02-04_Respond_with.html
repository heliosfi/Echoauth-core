Runtime Governance Wrapper


Scope & Safety Clarification (Condensed)


Purpose


This note clarifies the scope boundaries and safety properties of the proposed runtime governance wrapper to support accurate evaluation.


⸻


What the Wrapper Does


The runtime governance wrapper is a non-agent control layer that governs execution permission, not decision-making.


It:

• Enforces predefined, human-anchored invariants at execution time.

• Grants, holds, or denies execution based on resolved authority and validated system state.

• Treats uncertainty, ambiguity, or conflict as blocking conditions.

• Invalidates assumed or cached permissions when context changes.

• Produces auditable execution outcomes (grant / hold / deny).


The wrapper does not generate actions; it determines whether execution may proceed.


⸻


What the Wrapper Does Not Do


The wrapper does not:

• Generate goals, plans, recommendations, or predictions.

• Optimize outcomes, performance, or rewards.

• Learn, adapt, or update models.

• Resolve ethical or normative questions.

• Act autonomously or initiate execution.


It is not an AI agent, alignment mechanism, or decision system. It is a constraint-enforcing execution gate only.


⸻


Authority


Authority never resides in the wrapper.

• All authority is explicitly defined upstream and human-anchored.

• The wrapper does not infer, accumulate, or transfer authority.

• If authority is unclear, disputed, or absent, execution cannot proceed.


⸻


Halt Conditions


The wrapper enforces halt-by-default behavior when:

• Authority is unresolved or conflicting.

• Required invariants are unmet.

• Inputs are ambiguous, incomplete, or out of scope.

• Execution context deviates from authorization context.


In these cases, the wrapper prevents execution and defers to designated human authority.

Non-execution is an intended and correct outcome.


⸻


Summary


The runtime governance wrapper is a non-optimizing, non-agent execution constraint designed to prevent unsafe or unauthorized action under uncertainty by enforcing explicit, human-anchored invariants at runtime.
