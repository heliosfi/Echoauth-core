Reviewer Clarification: Delegation Drift and EchoAuth’s Core Contribution


Recent discussions in AI safety and deployment have highlighted a growing class of failures commonly described as delegation drift, in which responsibilities delegated to AI systems expand beyond their intended scope or degrade over time due to context loss, cached permissions, ambiguous authority, or insufficient enforcement mechanisms. Documented examples include scope creep from AI-generated artifacts, architectural drift in software systems, instability in multi-agent coordination, and security incidents involving unintended privilege escalation.


Most current mitigation approaches address these risks through continuous monitoring, human checkpoints, or policy-level authority boundaries. While valuable, these approaches remain observational or corrective in nature: they detect or respond to drift after unsafe conditions have already emerged.


EchoAuth addresses the root cause at a deeper architectural level. Rather than relying on oversight to catch failure, EchoAuth enforces authorization directly at the execution layer. Authority is machine-enforced, human-anchored, and validated in real time through presence and continuity checks. The system explicitly treats refusal as a valid and expected operational state, invalidates cached or historical permissions, prevents execution under ambiguity, and collapses agency entirely when authority is unclear rather than redistributing it.


As a result, unsafe actions are not merely flagged or reviewed—they are structurally prevented from occurring. This distinction is material: monitoring observes failure, whereas EchoAuth prevents it by construction.


EchoAuth is designed specifically for environments involving autistic children who cannot reliably self-authorize, and for the parents, caregivers, school staff, and clinical staff who are legally and ethically responsible for them. The system is non-diagnostic, does not infer intent or behavioral state, and does not automate human judgment. Its sole function is to ensure that actions affecting a protected individual only occur when verified human authority is present, current, and coherent across home, educational, and clinical contexts.


In this way, EchoAuth represents an authorization-first safety architecture rather than a model-centric or policy-centric safeguard, directly addressing the core failure modes underlying delegation drift in real-world systems.
