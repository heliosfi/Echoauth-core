1) EchoAuth-First Grant Language


(Clear, humane, funder-ready, non-diagnostic)


Project Summary


EchoAuth is a caregiver-anchored authorization system designed for autistic children—especially non-verbal or minimally verbal children—who cannot reliably self-authorize in digital or physical environments. The system supports parents, caregivers, school staff, and clinic staff by ensuring that actions affecting a child only occur when a responsible adult’s presence and authority are verified in real time.


Most existing systems assume individuals can authenticate themselves, express intent, or recover from errors. EchoAuth is built for families where that assumption does not hold. Instead of inferring intent or relying on static credentials, EchoAuth enforces presence, continuity, and role-based human authority, and it refuses action when authorization is unclear.


EchoAuth does not diagnose, classify, or assess a child’s condition. It functions solely as a human-safe authorization and coordination layer, preserving parental authority, reducing risk, and supporting safer participation across home, school, and clinical settings.


Intended Impact

• Reduce unsafe or inappropriate actions affecting autistic children

• Support parents and caregivers who carry constant responsibility

• Improve coordination between home, school, and clinics without increasing surveillance or automation risk

• Preserve dignity, safety, and human accountability

2) EchoAuth-First Pilot Framing


(Concrete, ethical, low-risk, defensible)


Pilot Goal


Demonstrate that EchoAuth can safely coordinate access and actions for autistic children by enforcing caregiver-anchored authorization and refusing action under uncertainty.


Pilot Environment

• Settings: Home, classroom, or clinical support space

• Participants:

• One autistic child (non-verbal or minimally verbal)

• Authorized adults (parent/guardian, caregiver, teacher, or clinic staff)


What the Pilot Tests

• Verification of authorized adult presence

• Continuity of authorization across time and setting

• System refusal when authorization is absent, broken, or ambiguous

• Clear logging and escalation to humans


What the Pilot Explicitly Does Not Do

• No diagnosis or behavioral assessment

• No prediction of intent

• No autonomous decision-making

• No replacement of caregiver or professional judgment


Success Criteria


The pilot is successful if:

• The system refuses action when caregiver authority is unclear

• All refusals and escalations are logged transparently

• No action is executed based on inference, history, or urgency


In this pilot, refusal is a success condition, not a failure.

3) EchoAuth-First Institutional Positioning


(For schools, clinics, districts, regulators, foundations)


What EchoAuth Is


EchoAuth is not an AI assistant and not a diagnostic tool.

It is an authorization and coordination system for environments where children cannot reliably self-authorize.


EchoAuth answers three institutional questions:

1. Who is present?

2. Who is authorized to act for this child?

3. Is it safe to proceed right now?


Who It Serves

• Autistic children who need protection, not inference

• Parents and legal guardians as primary authority holders

• Caregivers, school staff, and clinic staff operating within defined roles and boundaries


Why Institutions Use It

• Reduces liability from inappropriate automation

• Preserves parental and institutional accountability

• Aligns with safeguarding and duty-of-care obligations

• Works across home, school, and clinical contexts without diagnosis or surveillance


Institutional One-Line


EchoAuth enables safe coordination for autistic children by enforcing parent- and caregiver-anchored authorization across home, school, and clinical environments, while refusing action under uncertainty.
