What people still misunderstand about “frequencies” (EEG bands)


1) They think bands are “things,” not measurement buckets


Delta/theta/alpha/etc are human-made bins over a continuous spectrum. The brain doesn’t “enter alpha mode” like changing gears.


2) They ignore the aperiodic 1/f background


A huge amount of what looks like “more alpha” or “less beta” can actually be a change in the aperiodic (1/f-like) slope/offset, not a true oscillation change. If you don’t separate periodic peaks from aperiodic activity, you misread the graph.  


3) They treat “power” like it equals “function”


Power is amplitude energy at a frequency band — but function is often about timing (phase), synchrony, and coordination, not just “more/less.” That’s why two people can show similar “alpha power” but very different attention performance.


4) They underestimate cross-frequency coupling (the real “bridge”)


A lot of cognition is coordinated by interactions like theta phase modulating gamma amplitude, etc. People see separate bands; the brain uses relationships between them. But coupling metrics are easy to miscompute and misinterpret.  


5) They assume “gamma = higher consciousness” (common myth)


Gamma is noisy to measure at the scalp, easily contaminated by muscle activity, and not a simple “more is better” slider. “High frequency” is often where artifacts live.


6) They think graphs are brain-only (but EEG is brain + body + environment)


Eye blinks, jaw tension, movement, lighting, stress, sound, posture — these can dominate what the “frequency graph” looks like in real life. So the “state” isn’t just internal — it’s systems-level.

What humans miss about ASD + why many ASD devices “fail”


A lot of ASD tech fails because it tries to win on analysis instead of winning on stability.


Here are the recurring failure modes seen across assistive tech and AI-for-autism literature:


1) Overstimulating interfaces


Too many choices, movement, notifications, transitions → sensory overload and dropout. Sensory processing differences and attentional challenges are well documented in ASD.  


2) High cognitive demand


Many tools assume the user can comply with multi-step instructions, adapt to novel UI, or “learn the system.” For many users, that’s the exact wrong assumption.


3) One-size-fits-all personalization


ASD variability is huge (hyper/hypo sensitivities can even vary within the same person over time). Tools that don’t adapt to state and context break quickly.  


4) Caregiver burden + role confusion


If tools require constant setup, interpretation, or “manual enforcement,” caregivers burn out and the tool dies. Adoption barriers and usability limits are a known issue for digital health tech broadly, including caregiver-mediated tools.  


5) They drift into clinical territory


Once a product sounds like diagnosis/therapy/behavior scoring, it triggers regulatory friction, school hesitancy, and parent mistrust — even if the intention was supportive.
