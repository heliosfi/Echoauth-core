🧠 Reviewer Question 4


Opportunity Cost — “Why Not Fund Interpretability Instead?”


This is the hardest structural question.

If you answer competitively, you lose.

⸻


Refined EA-Grade Response


Interpretability reduces epistemic uncertainty about model cognition.


EchoAuth reduces execution uncertainty at runtime.


These address orthogonal failure classes.


Even a perfectly interpretable model can act under ambiguous authority if its execution channel is unconstrained. Interpretability improves understanding; it does not enforce authority binding at the moment of side effect.


Governance failures historically occur at the execution boundary, not at the reasoning layer.


EchoAuth is not a substitute for interpretability. It is a runtime containment layer that remains necessary even under strong alignment assumptions.


Defense-in-depth dominates single-point reliance in safety-critical systems.

Brain-level alignment without execution gating leaves the affordance surface open.


Why This Lands

• It does not compete with interpretability.

• It isolates a distinct failure class.

• It frames layered safety as dominant strategy.

• It avoids claiming superiority.


EA reviewers respect modular safety architecture.

🧠 Reviewer Question 5


Falsifiability — “How Do You Measure Silence?”


This is where you win or lose credibility.


If you say “audit logs,” that’s insufficient.


You must show counterfactual measurability.


⸻


Refined EA-Grade Response


EchoAuth produces observable, structured non-events.


Every prevented execution produces:

• a deterministic reason code,

• a validation step identifier,

• and an append-only audit record.


We do not measure “nothing happened.”

We measure blocked attempts and structural enforcement frequency.


The Minimum Audit Set provides binary falsifiability:

• If any SK-vector fails, the system does not satisfy its safety claim.

• If unauthorized execution occurs under a valid test condition, the claim collapses.


Additionally, deployment metrics include:

• frequency of HOLD/HALT events,

• replay attempts detected,

• scope mismatch incidents,

• expired grant usage attempts.


The system’s claim is falsified if:

• execution occurs without valid grant,

• nonce replay succeeds,

• instruction drift bypasses hash validation,

• or locks fail to serialize.


This is mechanical falsifiability, not behavioral inference.


Why This Lands

• It shifts from “nothing happened” to “prevented attempts logged.”

• It defines falsification triggers.

• It ties directly to test vectors and audit rows.

• It demonstrates binary pass/fail.


This is strong in EA context.
