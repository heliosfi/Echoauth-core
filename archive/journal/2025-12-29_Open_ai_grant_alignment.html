Human-Safe Authorization


Preventing AI Action Under Ambiguity Through Structural Alignment


Nicholas B. Carty


⸻


Abstract


As artificial intelligence systems increasingly assist or automate decisions, the primary safety risk is no longer computational error but premature action under ambiguity. Many failures attributed to AI “misalignment” arise not from malicious intent or insufficient intelligence, but from systems being permitted to act when authority, intent, or context is unclear. This paper introduces Human-Safe Authorization, a design principle that enforces structural pause, explicit authority, and deterministic decision boundaries before action is allowed. Rather than relying on post-hoc policies or human vigilance, Human-Safe Authorization embeds safety directly into system architecture. A reference implementation demonstrates how cognition-aligned, pause-capable systems can reduce exploitability, prevent drift, and preserve accountability in high-stakes AI-assisted environments.


⸻


1. The Misunderstood Risk in AI Systems


Current AI safety discourse often focuses on:

• model capability

• alignment objectives

• misuse through malicious actors


While important, these miss a pervasive failure mode: AI systems acting when they should not act.


In practice, many harmful outcomes occur when:

• probabilistic outputs are treated as decisions

• ambiguity is resolved through inference rather than clarification

• humans are pressured to approve actions under time or cognitive stress


The issue is not that systems lack intelligence, but that they lack structural restraint.


⸻


2. Intelligence Without Constraint Amplifies Risk


Modern AI systems excel at:

• pattern completion

• prediction under uncertainty

• adaptive response


These strengths become liabilities when systems are allowed to:

• fill gaps instead of pausing

• escalate actions without explicit human intent

• operate across blurred authority boundaries


Increasing model capability without increasing action constraints widens the failure surface.


Human-Safe Authorization reframes safety as a control-system problem, not a prediction problem.


⸻


3. Human Limits Are Structural, Not Behavioral


Many AI-assisted systems assume humans can:

• monitor continuously

• interpret probabilistic outputs correctly

• intervene under stress

• maintain perfect situational awareness


Research across cognitive science and human factors shows this assumption is false. Under load, ambiguity, or time pressure, even trained operators make errors.


A safe system must absorb uncertainty, not externalize it to the human.


⸻


4. Core Principle: Stability Before Action


Human-Safe Authorization is governed by one invariant:


No system action may occur until authority, intent, and context are unambiguous.


This requires three architectural commitments:

1. Pause as a first-class system state

2. Explicit authority separation enforced in code

3. Deterministic transitions from intent to action


These are structural guarantees, not policies.


⸻


5. Pause Is a Safety Mechanism, Not Latency


Most digital systems treat delay as inefficiency.

Human-Safe Authorization treats delay as containment.


A pause:

• prevents irreversible action

• forces clarification upstream

• protects operators from coercive urgency

• preserves auditability


Physical safety systems already rely on this principle. AI systems must adopt it as well.


⸻


6. Structural Authority Over Procedural Oversight


Many AI systems rely on procedural safeguards:

• approvals

• policies

• checklists


These fail under stress.


Human-Safe Authorization requires structural authority, meaning:

• roles are enforced by the system, not by memory

• no hidden override paths exist

• deny-by-default behavior is enforced when policy is unclear


Authority becomes unskippable.


⸻


7. Why Ambiguity Is the Primary Exploit Surface


Advanced AI misuse does not require breaking encryption or security primitives. It exploits:

• interpretive gaps

• probabilistic outputs framed as certainty

• silent state transitions

• over-trust in automated recommendations


By collapsing ambiguity through bounded states, explicit confirmation, and deterministic enforcement, Human-Safe Authorization reduces the surface area available for both accidental and adversarial misuse.


⸻


8. Reference Implementation (Overview)


A working reference system demonstrates this principle by:

• stabilizing the user interface before authorization

• separating recognition from verification

• limiting user actions to a single confirmable intent

• enforcing backend policy deterministically

• logging all outcomes immutably


Complexity exists only behind the scenes.

The human-facing layer remains calm, predictable, and bounded.


This architecture has proven resilient in contexts where conventional AI-assisted systems fail due to stress, variability, or non-verbal interaction.


⸻


9. Relevance to AI Alignment and Safety Research


Human-Safe Authorization contributes to AI safety by:

• reducing reliance on model “correctness” under uncertainty

• preventing escalation without explicit human intent

• preserving clear accountability

• aligning system behavior with human cognitive limits


It complements, rather than replaces, existing alignment techniques.


⸻


Conclusion


AI safety failures are increasingly failures of authorization, not intelligence. Systems that cannot pause under uncertainty, respect authority boundaries, or constrain action structurally will continue to produce harmful outcomes as capabilities scale.


Human-Safe Authorization offers a principled, architecture-level approach to preventing such failures by embedding restraint directly into system design.


⸻


Closing Statement


A system that cannot refuse to act under ambiguity is not aligned—it is unsafe.
