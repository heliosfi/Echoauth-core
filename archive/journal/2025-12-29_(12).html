Human-Safe Authorization


Why AI Systems Must Not Act Under Ambiguity


Nicholas B. Carty


(Guardian Architect of Human–System Boundaries)


⸻


Abstract


As artificial intelligence systems increasingly assist or automate decisions in high-stakes environments—defense, finance, justice, healthcare, and public infrastructure—the dominant risk is no longer computational error, but action taken under ambiguity. Many system failures arise not because machines lack intelligence, but because they are permitted to act when authority, intent, or context is unclear. This paper proposes a design principle—Human-Safe Authorization—that prioritizes stability, explicit authority, and pause-on-uncertainty over speed or autonomy. Rather than asking humans to adapt to increasingly complex systems, Human-Safe Authorization requires systems to align with human cognitive and institutional limits. A reference implementation demonstrates how bounded, pause-capable architectures reduce exploitability, prevent drift, and maintain accountability under stress.


⸻


1. The Real Failure Mode of Modern Systems


Modern AI failures rarely originate from a single bug or malicious actor. Instead, they emerge from compound uncertainty:

• unclear authority boundaries

• probabilistic inference presented as certainty

• adaptive systems filling gaps rather than pausing

• humans pressured to approve under time stress


When systems act in these conditions, responsibility becomes diffuse, errors propagate silently, and post-incident accountability collapses.


The dominant design assumption has been:


If the system is intelligent enough, it can act safely.


This assumption is false in high-stakes contexts.


⸻


2. Intelligence Is Not the Primary Risk


AI systems today are excellent at:

• pattern recognition

• prediction under stable distributions

• optimization within defined parameters


They are poor at:

• recognizing when not to act

• respecting authority separation

• detecting context collapse

• managing responsibility boundaries


Increasing intelligence without increasing constraint amplifies risk.


The problem is not insufficient intelligence.

The problem is unbounded action.


⸻


3. Human Systems Fail Under Cognitive Load


Human operators fail when:

• interfaces are overloaded

• decisions branch excessively

• urgency overrides verification

• responsibility is unclear


These failures are not moral or intellectual; they are structural.


Systems that assume constant human vigilance or perfect decision-making under stress are inherently unsafe.


A human-safe system must absorb uncertainty, not transfer it to the operator.


⸻


4. The Core Principle: Stability Before Action


Human-Safe Authorization rests on one invariant:


No system may act until authority, intent, and context are unambiguous.


This requires:

• Stability before execution

• Explicit authority before escalation

• Pause as a first-class system state


Action is permitted only after these conditions are satisfied.


This is not a policy choice; it is an architectural requirement.


⸻


5. Pause Is Not Failure—It Is Safety


Most systems treat delay as inefficiency.

Human-Safe Authorization treats delay as containment.


A pause state:

• prevents irreversible action

• forces upstream clarification

• preserves auditability

• protects operators from coercive urgency


In aviation, nuclear systems, and physical safety engineering, pause-on-uncertainty is already standard. Digital and AI systems lag behind.


⸻


6. Authority Must Be Structural, Not Procedural


Many systems rely on procedural authority:

• checklists

• approvals

• policies layered on top of autonomous systems


These fail under stress.


Human-Safe Authorization requires structural authority:

• role separation enforced in code

• no hidden override paths

• deny-by-default when policy is unclear


Authority must be unskippable, not advisory.


⸻


7. Why AI-Driven Exploits Target Ambiguity


Advanced AI systems exploit:

• probabilistic gaps

• pattern-based inference

• silent state transitions

• human trust in “smart” outputs


They do not need to break cryptography.

They exploit interpretive space.


By removing ambiguity—through fixed states, explicit confirmation, and deterministic transitions—Human-Safe Authorization collapses the exploit surface.


⸻


8. Reference Implementation: Cognition-Aligned Authorization


A working reference system demonstrates this principle by:

• stabilizing the interface before authentication

• separating recognition from verification

• limiting action to single, confirmable steps

• enforcing backend policy deterministically

• logging all outcomes immutably


Complexity exists only behind the scenes.

The human-facing layer remains calm, predictable, and bounded.


This approach has proven resilient in environments where traditional systems fail due to stress, variability, or non-verbal interaction.


⸻


9. Applicability Across Domains


Human-Safe Authorization is relevant wherever:

• decisions have irreversible consequences

• authority is distributed

• environments are stressful or unstable

• AI assists or accelerates action


This includes:

• defense and national security

• finance and banking

• justice and law enforcement

• healthcare access systems

• emergency response and disaster relief


The principle scales because it reduces—not increases—assumptions about human performance.


⸻


10. Reframing Progress


Progress in intelligent systems should not be measured by:

• speed of action

• autonomy

• prediction accuracy alone


It must also be measured by:

• refusal to act when uncertain

• clarity of authority

• survivability under stress


Systems that cannot pause are unsafe by design.


⸻


Conclusion


Human-Safe Authorization is not an argument against AI.

It is an argument for responsible boundaries.


As systems grow more capable, the cost of premature or unauthorized action grows exponentially. The safest systems of the future will not be those that act fastest, but those that know when not to act.


Stability before action is not a limitation.

It is the foundation of trust.


⸻


Closing Statement


A system that cannot pause under uncertainty is not intelligent—it is dangerous.
