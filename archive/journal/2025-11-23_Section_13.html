⭐ PATENT SECTION 13 — ENVIRONMENT RISK DETECTION ENGINE (ERDE)


(This is a sub-module inside MESE, but powerful enough to stand as its own patent component.)


────────────────────────


⭐ 13.1 Overview


The Environment Risk Detection Engine (ERDE) evaluates the safety, sensory load, and behavioral danger potential of the environment surrounding a neurodivergent individual.


It identifies physical, sensory, emotional, crowd-related, and transition-based risks using a combination of:

• environmental sensors

• motion data

• behavioral cues

• sensory volatility

• NI–AI fusion rules

• learned historical patterns


Unlike conventional safety tools that rely solely on GPS or motion limits, ERDE predicts danger before it materializes.


────────────────────────


⭐ 13.2 Inputs to ERDE


ERDE integrates multiple categories of environmental information:


A. Physical Environment Inputs

• proximity to streets

• open-door conditions

• unguarded exits

• dangerous objects

• clutter density

• high movement areas


B. Sensory Environment Inputs


(Provided by SODE)

• noise spikes

• brightness fluctuations

• motion density

• temperature shifts

• crowd clustering


C. Behavioral Indicators


(Provided by BPE)

• pacing toward exits

• exploratory leaning

• agitation near unsafe zones

• sudden directional movement

• freeze-before-flight patterns


D. Environmental Metadata


Automatically tagged:

• “home”

• “school”

• “transportation”

• “community”

• “medical setting”


E. NI Context Rules


Caregiver-specified risk zones:

• “he runs toward doors”

• “parking lots overwhelm him”

• “crowds trigger flight mode”


This makes the system CHILD-SPECIFIC.


────────────────────────


⭐ 13.3 Environment Risk Processing Pipeline


ERDE processes risk in four sequential steps:


Step 1: Hazard Recognition

• open/closing door detection

• street proximity detection

• edge detection (stairs, railings)

• unsafe objects (kitchen, sharp items)


Step 2: Sensory Danger Amplification


If sensory load is high → risk level increases

If environmental volatility is high → cognitive stability decreases


Step 3: Behavioral Risk Correlation


If pacing increases near unsafe areas

→ danger↑

If stillness precedes wandering patterns

→ danger↑

If stimming spikes when passing crowds

→ danger↑


Step 4: NI–AI Fusion Interpretation


The parent knows patterns the AI cannot:


Examples:

• “He freezes before running.”

• “She hides before eloping.”

• “He bolts when overwhelmed.”


NI modifies thresholds and triggers.


────────────────────────


⭐ 13.4 Risk Classification Levels


ERDE outputs five danger levels:


LEVEL 1 — Safe Zone


Calm and predictable.


LEVEL 2 — Mild Risk Zone


Some sensory volatility or mild movement patterns.


LEVEL 3 — Moderate Risk Zone


Crowds, noise changes, pacing behavior.


LEVEL 4 — High Risk Zone


Proximity to exits, streets, open doors, rapid movement.


LEVEL 5 — Critical Danger


Elopement probable within seconds.


Each level triggers different interventions.


────────────────────────


⭐ 13.5 Integration With Other Engines


ERDE is directly tied to:

• MESE (Multi-Environment Safety Engine)

• SODE (Sensory Overload Engine)

• BPE (Behavior Prediction Engine)

• NEDE (Nonverbal Expression Engine)

These inputs allow ERDE to produce accurate, multi-dimensional safety judgments.


────────────────────────


⭐ 13.6 Outputs of ERDE


Outputs include:

• Risk Level (1–5)

• Environmental Hazard Type (door, street, crowd, noise, etc.)

• Recommended Caregiver Action

• Intervention Prompt

• Projected Risk Timeline

• Environment Adjustment Suggestions


Examples:

• “High exit risk detected — stay within arm’s reach.”

• “Noise spike + pacing → potential overload.”

• “Crowd movement increasing → move child to quieter area.”
