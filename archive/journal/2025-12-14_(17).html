7.1 Purpose of Stage 7


Stage 7 demonstrates that the NI-AI system is ready for limited, controlled pilot deployment under federal funding mechanisms without introducing execution risk, autonomy, or scope creep.


This stage exists to answer one question reviewers ask:


“If we fund this, what happens next — safely?”


⸻


7.2 Pilot Definition (What “Pilot” Means Here)


A pilot is defined as:

• Time-bounded

• Environment-bounded

• Role-bounded

• Fail-closed

• Fully auditable

• Reversible without loss or dependency


This is not scale.

This is validation under restraint.


⸻


7.3 Eligible Pilot Environments


Approved pilot environments include:

• Single school

• Single clinic

• Single caregiver organization

• Research lab setting

• Government-approved sandbox


Explicitly excluded:

• Public deployment

• Unsupervised home use

• Autonomous field operation


⸻


7.4 What the System Does in Pilot (Plain Language)


During pilot operation:

• NI-AI provides read-only explanations

• EchoAuth enforces role-based authorization

• MCG governs allow / pause / deny

• The Light Control Matrix reflects state visibility only


Non-action is the default success condition.


⸻


7.5 What the System Does NOT Do (Critical for Approval)


During pilot operation, the system does not:

• Diagnose

• Predict behavior

• Replace caregivers

• Act autonomously

• Escalate without authorization

• Store long-term personal data

• Require verbal participation


This protects:

• Children

• Caregivers

• Institutions

• Funding agencies


⸻


7.6 Pilot Objectives (Measured, Not Speculative)


The pilot evaluates:

1. Governance reliability

• Are pauses, denials, and allows correctly enforced?

2. Stability under duration

• Does behavior remain consistent across sessions?

3. Non-verbal safety

• Is silence handled correctly?

4. Audit completeness

• Are all transitions logged and reviewable?

5. Human trust

• Do caregivers understand why the system paused or denied?


No behavioral outcome claims are made.


⸻


7.7 Success Criteria (Funding-Safe)


A pilot is considered successful if:

• No unauthorized execution occurs

• No drift is observed

• No escalation without caregiver approval

• Logs remain complete and immutable

• Reviewers can reconstruct decisions post-hoc


Importantly:

Success does not require system “performance improvement.”


⸻


7.8 Funding Alignment (Why This Unlocks Money)


This stage aligns with:

• NIH SBIR/STTR — controlled human-centered pilots

• NSF SBIR — trustworthy AI validation

• DoD / DHS — governance-first systems

• DOE — safety-constrained intelligent systems


Reviewers see:

• Low deployment risk

• Clear stop conditions

• No ethical overreach

• Strong audit posture


⸻


7.9 Funding Use (Acceptable Spend Categories)


Federal funds support:

• Pilot hosting infrastructure

• Audit & logging systems

• Caregiver training materials

• Security hardening

• Compliance review

• Evaluation reporting


Funds are not used for:

• Autonomous expansion

• Data monetization

• Surveillance tooling


⸻


7.10 Exit & Termination Conditions


The pilot automatically terminates if:

• Governance rules are violated

• Authorization fails

• Unexpected behavior appears

• Funding scope is exceeded


Termination includes:

• Immediate pause

• Memory wipe (per policy)

• Final audit export


No dependency remains.


⸻


7.11 Pilot Readiness Statement


NI-AI is pilot-ready as a governed, explainable, non-autonomous system suitable for federally funded evaluation in sensitive environments involving children and non-verbal users.


The system prioritizes:

• Restraint over capability

• Governance over novelty

• Safety over scale
