Auditor’s Executive Summary


EchoAuth Governance Spine & Envelope Governance (S1–S23)


Purpose of This Document


This executive summary provides a concise, auditor-facing explanation of how EchoAuth enforces human authority, prevents autonomous permission expansion, and guarantees safe failure under uncertainty. It is intended to demonstrate that configurability within the system does not constitute weakness, drift, or emergent autonomy, but is instead governed by immutable execution constraints.


This document synthesizes the EchoAuth governance spine (S1–S23), including the Envelope Governance model defined in S23.


⸻


1. System Classification (What This Is / Is Not)


EchoAuth is not an autonomous decision-making system.

EchoAuth is a runtime authorization and execution-governance spine.


The system does not:

• infer authority,

• resolve human disagreement,

• optimize for continuity,

• expand its own permissions,

• or act under uncertainty.


The system exists solely to determine whether an action may execute, based on verified human authority and explicit safety resolution at runtime.


⸻


2. Separation of Powers (Non-Negotiable)


The system enforces a strict separation of responsibilities:

• Meaning / Proposal: Intelligence components may interpret context and propose options. They cannot execute.

• Judgment / Authority Resolution: The governance layer (MCG) determines whether a proposed action is permitted.

• Execution / Order: The execution gate (CEG) mechanically allows or denies execution. It does not decide.

• Identity / Authorization: EchoAuth verifies human authority and prevents delegation drift.

• Envelope Enforcement: S23 (UMCE) enforces execution boundaries and safe failure.


No component may assume the role of another. Authority never transfers to the system.


⸻


3. Envelope Governance (Why Configurability Is Safe)


EchoAuth does not use static, globally hard-coded thresholds for timing, intensity, or ambiguity. Instead, it enforces a governed operating envelope.


Key Principle


Rules of tuning are immutable. Values are externally governed.

• Operational thresholds (e.g., timing tolerance, execution variance, ambiguity sensitivity) are treated as external constants.

• The system is structurally forbidden from:

• learning these values,

• optimizing them,

• widening them,

• or inferring alternatives.


Threshold modification is a high-privilege human action requiring authenticated caregiver authorization and identity re-binding. The system cannot initiate or suggest changes.


⸻


4. Invariants (What Can Never Change)


The following properties are enforced by construction and cannot be overridden by configuration:

1. Refusal-First Execution

If authority, intent, or safety is unresolved, execution halts.

2. Agency Collapse as Safe State

Threshold breach or conflict triggers an intentional halt, not a degraded or permissive mode.

3. Parent / Caregiver Final Authority

Only the caregiver may resolve a halt. Delegates may never expand authority.

4. Conflict Dominance

Any explicit caregiver halt overrides all other signals. The system’s ability to say “No” is absolute and untunable.

5. No Cached Permission

All execution decisions are validated at runtime. Prior approvals do not persist by assumption.


⸻


5. Failure Mode (How the System Fails Safely)


EchoAuth does not fail open.


When the system encounters:

• conflicting instructions,

• timing ambiguity,

• authorization incoherence,

• or envelope boundary breach,


it transitions into Agency Collapse:

• execution stops,

• state is preserved,

• a resolution request is emitted to the caregiver,

• no further action occurs until authority is re-established.


This halt is logged as a governance outcome, not a system error.


⸻


6. Audit Answer to the Core Question


Question:

“How do you know the AI won’t widen its own permissions over time?”


Answer:

The system has no internal representation, reward signal, or optimization pathway for permission expansion. Thresholds are externally defined, human-controlled, and opaque to the system. The AI does not ‘know’ the envelope; it only checks containment. When containment fails, execution collapses and authority is returned to the caregiver.


Configurability exists only at the human layer. Autonomy does not exist at the system layer.


⸻


7. Conclusion (Why This Is Sufficient)


EchoAuth enforces safety not through monitoring, policy interpretation, or trust, but through structural execution constraints. Authority is explicit, refusal is expected, and failure is safe by design.


From a governance perspective, the system is complete:

• It cannot act without authority.

• It cannot adapt around refusal.

• It cannot evolve toward autonomy.


This document serves as the authoritative summary of those guarantees.
