EchoAuth


A Deterministic Runtime Governance Primitive for Agentic Systems


Executive Summary


Core Claim

EchoAuth reduces a neglected class of AI risk: execution uncertainty. As AI systems gain real-world affordances, the primary safety failure shifts from misprediction to unauthorized action. EchoAuth enforces authority resolution before execution, making side effects mechanically contingent on verifiable human authorization.


Problem Framing

Most safety research focuses on cognition-layer alignment (interpretability, training objectives, behavior filtering). These approaches reduce epistemic uncertainty about what a model is thinking. They do not inherently prevent action under ambiguous or stale authority.


Even well-aligned systems can act when they should not if the execution boundary is unconstrained.


EchoAuth addresses the Authority-to-Action Gap at runtime.


⸻


Mechanism


EchoAuth implements a deterministic separation of powers:

• Identity Binding (UMCE) — establishes actor and role context.

• Judgment Layer (MCG) — resolves authority into a decision.

• Execution Gate (CEG) — performs no reasoning; executes only upon validation of a cryptographically bound, single-use authorization grant.


Every governed action requires:

• a fresh, time-bounded grant,

• instruction hash binding,

• state-lock acquisition,

• signature validation,

• atomic nonce commitment.


If any invariant fails, execution does not proceed.


Default outcomes:

• Ambiguity → HOLD

• Authority channel loss → HOLD

• Replay attempt → DENY

• Integrity breach → HALT


There is no “best-effort” execution path.


⸻


Falsifiability


EchoAuth makes binary, testable claims.


The system fails if:

• execution occurs without a valid grant,

• a replayed nonce succeeds,

• instruction drift bypasses hash validation,

• expired authority executes,

• concurrent actions bypass lock serialization.


These claims are verified through:

• a deterministic validation contract (8-step runtime sequence),

• typed API schema (Protobuf),

• adversarial test vectors (“Spine-Kill” suite),

• conformance checklist and minimum audit set.


Impact is measured through structured non-events:

• frequency of blocked unauthorized attempts,

• replay detections,

• scope mismatches,

• integrity breach logs.


The absence of unauthorized execution is demonstrable via audit artifacts.


⸻


Opportunity Cost Position


EchoAuth does not compete with interpretability or alignment research.


Interpretability reduces uncertainty about model cognition.

EchoAuth reduces uncertainty at the execution boundary.


These address orthogonal failure classes.


Defense-in-depth dominates single-layer safety.


⸻


Non-Capabilities


EchoAuth:

• does not enhance model intelligence,

• does not generate actions,

• does not centralize authority,

• does not automate delegation,

• does not override root authority.


It is a restrictive runtime primitive.


⸻


Theory of Change

1. Prove deterministic execution gating in high-stakes domain.

2. Publish open validation contract and test vectors.

3. Establish runtime authority binding as compliance expectation.

4. Increase legitimacy cost for unconstrained agentic systems.


EchoAuth scales by raising the baseline requirement for execution governance.


⸻


Summary


EchoAuth is not a smarter AI system.

It is a mechanical governance constraint designed to narrow the affordance window of agentic systems.


Safety is not improved through prediction or intent inference.

It is enforced at the point of action.
