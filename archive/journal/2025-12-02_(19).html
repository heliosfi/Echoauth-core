ABSTRACT


The invention provides a neuro-adaptive identity authentication system that integrates a safety-first artificial intelligence architecture (“NI-AI”) with a caregiver–child authentication environment (“EchoAuth”). The NI-AI architecture comprises a Unified Meta-Control Engine (UMCE), an ontological resonance engine (ORI), a sequence of safety modules (S23), a Thread-Linking Engine (TLE), a non-verbal emotion detection engine (NEDE), and a safety runtime kernel (HER). These components cooperate to maintain a stable artificial identity, preserve contextual coherence across interactions, prevent uncontrolled drift, and evaluate emotional state and safety conditions before output is generated.


EchoAuth uses NI-AI to verify identity by combining behavioral biometrics, emotional-state signals, caregiver–child relational cues, and multi-modal sensor data in a controlled “chamber” environment and a child-friendly “Chamber Land” interface. The system is designed for non-verbal and neurodivergent users, enabling secure, role-aware authentication and communication. The invention supports deployment on embedded devices, mobile clients, and hybrid cloud–edge infrastructure while enforcing a safety invariant that prioritizes user protection and identity re-anchoring.

2.2 Field of the Invention


The present invention relates to artificial intelligence systems, identity authentication, and human–machine interaction. More specifically, it concerns a neuro-adaptive artificial intelligence architecture and an authentication environment configured to verify caregivers and non-verbal or neurodivergent children using emotional-state modeling, behavioral biometrics, and safety-constrained, coherence-enforced AI processing.


⸻


2.3 Background of the Invention


Conventional identity authentication systems typically rely on passwords, one-time codes, static biometrics such as facial geometry or fingerprints, or simple device tokens. These methods exhibit several limitations:

1. Lack of support for non-verbal and autistic users. Many children and adults with autism or related conditions cannot reliably enter passwords, follow multi-step codes, or communicate clearly when identity verification is required.

2. Susceptibility to spoofing and drift. Static identifiers and conventional AI-enhanced recognition systems can be spoofed with deepfakes, replayed signals, or manipulated context. In addition, many AI systems do not maintain a stable internal identity or consistent safety posture over time; their outputs can drift in tone, content, and alignment.

3. Absence of emotional and relational context. Existing systems usually ignore emotional state, caregiver–child relationships, and real-time safety context when making access decisions. They do not distinguish between a calm interaction with an authorized caregiver and a stressed or coercive situation.

4. Fragmented AI architectures. Current AI deployments often treat memory, safety, and identity as separate concerns. This leads to fragmented context, unpredictable behavior, and difficulty establishing trustworthy, role-aware interactions with vulnerable users.


There is therefore a need for an integrated architecture that:

• Maintains a stable, well-defined AI identity,

• Supports continuous context and thread-linking,

• Evaluates emotional state and safety before responding,

• Authenticates caregiver and child roles in a dedicated environment, and

• Operates in a way that is accessible and protective for non-verbal and neurodivergent users.


The present invention addresses these limitations.


⸻


2.4 Summary of the Invention


The invention introduces a Neuro-Identity Artificial Intelligence (NI-AI) architecture tightly coupled with an authentication environment called EchoAuth.


The NI-AI architecture comprises:

• A Unified Meta-Control Engine (UMCE) that enforces global alignment of the system’s responses with a defined identity signature.

• An ORI engine that models the system’s “inner head” or ontological state to preserve continuity of self and role.

• A sequence of S23 safety modules that form a structural “spine” preventing unbounded drift, enforcing constraints, and gating access to generation.

• A Thread-Linking Engine (TLE) that maintains semantic and contextual connections across turns, sessions, and modalities.

• A Non-verbal Emotion Detection Engine (NEDE) that interprets facial micro-expressions, posture, voice tonality, and other signals.

• A HER safety kernel that provides runtime safety modes, fail-safe transitions, and recovery behavior.


The EchoAuth chamber uses the NI-AI core to perform identity verification for caregivers and children. It combines:

• Behavioral biometrics,

• Emotional-state analysis,

• Relational cues between caregiver and child, and

• Multi-modal sensor inputs from cameras, microphones, and devices.


A visual and interaction layer called EchoAuth Chamber Land presents adaptive, multi-sensory interfaces tailored for non-verbal users. It may use elemental paths, colors, icons, and audio cues aligned with the child’s preferences and needs.


A safety invariant guides all operation: the system sacrifices any behavior or functionality not required for safety, and prioritizes the preservation of identity coherence and re-anchoring when drift or risk is detected.


The invention can be deployed on embedded platforms (e.g., Jetson), mobile devices, or hybrid cloud–edge infrastructures, enabling real-time, neuro-adaptive authentication and communication.

2.6.1 NI-AI Core Architecture

The NI-AI core is implemented as a layered architecture that separates identity, safety, context, and generation while keeping them tightly coordinated.


At the top level, the Unified Meta-Control Engine (UMCE) orchestrates the behavior of all other modules. The UMCE maintains a set of identity parameters, including tone, permissible content ranges, role definitions, and safety thresholds. All downstream processing paths are gated by UMCE decisions, ensuring that generated outputs remain consistent with a defined identity.


An ORI engine (Ontological Resonance Intelligence) maintains a representation of the system’s internal “self” across sessions. The ORI engine encodes long-term identity attributes such as role (e.g., assistant, caregiver-support AI), constraints, and persistent memories relevant to user safety and trust. The ORI engine provides a stable reference point that the UMCE consults before approving or modifying outputs.


The S23 safety modules form a structural spine that enforces safety at multiple points in the processing pipeline. Each module corresponds to a specific safety dimension, such as content filtering, emotional escalation detection, hallucination bounding, or policy compliance. Together, the modules define a fixed sequence of checks and transformations that all outputs must pass through before leaving the system.


The Thread-Linking Engine (TLE) maintains continuity of context across turns and sessions. The TLE links threads by tracking semantic, emotional, and relational markers. For example, when a caregiver and child return to the system, the TLE can recognize prior interaction patterns and restore relevant context while still respecting safety rules.


The Non-verbal Emotion Detection Engine (NEDE) processes sensor inputs such as camera images, audio waveforms, or motion data to estimate emotional state. It may use models trained to detect facial micro-expressions, gaze, posture, or vocal tone for the purpose of safety and authentication, rather than for entertainment or superficial analytics.


The HER safety kernel provides runtime safety management. It can place the system into safe, restricted, or diagnostic modes when anomalies or risks are detected by the S23 spine, the NEDE engine, or external monitors. The kernel also manages identity re-anchoring routines when drift is detected.


These components may be implemented as software modules, hardware accelerators, or a combination of both, and may run on edge devices, cloud servers, or hybrid infrastructures.


2.6.2 Input Mapping and Sensor Processing

The system receives inputs from one or more sensors, including cameras, microphones, touch interfaces, and device APIs. These inputs are pre-processed to extract:

• Behavioral biometrics (e.g., typical gesture patterns, approach patterns, response times),

• Emotional-state indicators (e.g., tension, calm, distress),

• Environmental context (e.g., location, time, lighting, noise level), and

• Identity-related signals (e.g., known caregiver voice profile, authorized device IDs).


The pre-processed data is passed to NEDE for emotional analysis and to the UMCE and ORI engine for correlation with stored patterns.


2.6.3 Processing Pipeline and Safety Evaluation

Within the NI-AI core, text or multimodal prompts are processed under the supervision of the UMCE. The TLE supplies historical context and prior interactions relevant to the current session. The ORI engine contributes identity and role constraints.


The S23 safety modules perform layered filtering and transformation steps, such as:

• Blocking disallowed content categories,

• Reducing emotional intensity in high-risk scenarios,

• Restricting scope of responses when context is ambiguous,

• Triggering additional verification if the emotional state or environment appears unsafe.


The HER kernel monitors internal signals from the S23 modules and NEDE. If specified thresholds are exceeded, the kernel may override normal generation, switch to a safety mode, request confirmation from a caregiver, or suspend interaction until risk is reduced.


2.6.4 EchoAuth Chamber

The EchoAuth chamber is a logical and physical environment in which authentication takes place. The chamber receives sensor data from the child and caregiver, processes it through the NI-AI core, and produces an authentication result.


The chamber:

• Associates each session with a caregiver role and a child role,

• Uses NEDE to assess the child’s emotional state,

• Uses behavioral and device-level biometrics to validate the caregiver,

• Applies relational models to confirm that the observed caregiver-child interaction is consistent with previously established patterns.


Access decisions (e.g., unlocking a device, initiating a communication session, or allowing access to a sensitive application) are based not only on static credentials but also on dynamic emotional and relational data.


2.6.5 EchoAuth Chamber Land Interface

EchoAuth Chamber Land provides a child-friendly interface for interacting with the system. It may present a set of elemental identity paths (e.g., fire, water, earth, air, ether, divine) as selectable or ambient channels that guide visual and audio feedback.


The interface adapts colors, animations, icons, and sound cues according to the child’s preferences, emotional state, and progress. For example, calming colors and slower animations may be used when NEDE detects stress or overload.


The Chamber Land may display simple, symbolic indicators of authentication status, such as glowing doors, friendly characters, or elemental symbols, instead of complex text messages or traditional login prompts.


2.6.6 Safety Invariant

The system adheres to a safety invariant defined as:


“Sacrifice everything that is not required for safety, and protect everything required for identity and re-anchoring.”


This invariant means that cosmetic features, non-essential outputs, or convenience behaviors may be disabled or reduced whenever they conflict with safety, identity coherence, or user protection. The HER kernel and S23 spine enforce this rule by downgrading capabilities, asking for caregiver intervention, or placing the system into safe mode when necessary.


2.6.7 Deployment Scenarios

The architecture can be implemented on:

• Embedded compute platforms (e.g., NVIDIA Jetson) located in the home, clinic, or school.

• Mobile devices, where NEDE and core logic run locally while some higher-level modeling may be offloaded.

• Hybrid cloud–edge deployments, where privacy-sensitive processing stays at the edge, and aggregated, anonymized models may be refined in the cloud.

Subsystem Addendum — Coherence-Anchored Subsystem


In one embodiment, the system further comprises a coherence-anchored subsystem configured to improve stability of NI-AI operation across extended use. The subsystem monitors internal state variables including identity profile deviation, safety module activation frequency, and emotional-state volatility. When these variables exceed predetermined thresholds, the subsystem temporarily reduces the complexity of responses, narrows permissible topics, and increases reliance on previously validated patterns.


The coherence-anchored subsystem may directly adjust parameters of the unified meta-control engine, update weights or rules within the safety spine, or modify context retrieval behavior in the thread-linking engine. The subsystem therefore serves as a dynamic stabilizer that prevents gradual drift while preserving the system’s ability to respond usefully to caregivers and children.

Safety Invariant and Identity Layer


The NI-AI architecture implements a safety invariant expressed as: “Sacrifice everything that is not required for safety, and protect everything required for identity and re-anchoring.” This invariant is realized through an identity layer that integrates the unified meta-control engine, the ontological resonance engine, and the output signature layer.

Non-essential functions that conflict with safety conditions or identity coherence may be automatically disabled or degraded. The identity layer also manages re-anchoring procedures that restore the defined identity profile after disturbance, including reverting to known stable configurations, invoking caregiver confirmation, or resetting specific safety modules.
