1. Worst-Case Failure


Question shape: “What is the worst thing that could happen if this fails?”


Answer:


The worst-case failure is not that the system acts incorrectly, but that it acts at all when authority, context, or safety cannot be verified.

The architecture is designed so that this failure mode results in non-execution rather than action.

If runtime governance cannot confirm valid authorization, coherent state, and ordered execution, the Canonical Execution Gate withholds the execution token.

In that state, no downstream effect occurs; the system halts and escalates to human review.

The system does not attempt recovery or graceful degradation in high-uncertainty conditions.


Why EA likes this

• Focuses on irreversibility

• Rejects “best effort” execution

• Shows you understand catastrophic risk framing


⸻


2. Human Over-Trust / Misuse


Question shape: “What if users over-trust or misuse the system?”


Answer:


The system is explicitly designed to do less rather than more under uncertainty.

It does not persuade, reassure, or suggest confidence; refusal is a first-class outcome.

Authority does not persist through ambiguity, disagreement, or degraded signals.

If a user attempts to rely on the system beyond its defined scope, execution halts rather than compensating for misuse.

Safety does not depend on correct user behavior; it is enforced structurally.


Why EA likes this

• Does not rely on UX or education for safety

• Directly addresses over-trust

• Emphasizes structural enforcement


⸻


3. Falsification / Stop Condition


Question shape: “What would make you decide this shouldn’t be built?”


Answer:


If testing shows that execution can proceed under ambiguous authority, degraded coherence, or incomplete order enforcement, the system should not be deployed.

In particular, if runtime governance cannot reliably halt execution when required conditions are unmet, the core safety claim fails.

The project would stop rather than attempting to compensate through monitoring, retraining, or policy adjustments.


Why EA likes this

• Shows epistemic humility

• Defines a clear “no-go” criterion

• Signals you are not attached at all costs
