EA Funds–Focused Clarification: Delegation Drift and EchoAuth’s Safety Contribution


A growing concern in applied AI safety is delegation drift: situations in which responsibilities delegated to AI systems expand beyond their intended scope or degrade over time due to context loss, cached permissions, ambiguous authority, or insufficient enforcement. This failure mode is increasingly visible across AI-assisted software development, agentic systems, enterprise copilots, and security incidents involving unintended privilege escalation.


Most existing mitigations focus on monitoring and oversight—for example, continuous human review, checkpoints, or policy-level authority boundaries. While these approaches can detect or respond to problems, they generally operate after unsafe conditions have already arisen and rely on sustained human vigilance.


EchoAuth addresses delegation drift at a deeper, structural level. Instead of improving observation, it constrains what systems are permitted to execute at all. EchoAuth enforces human-anchored authorization directly at runtime, making authority a machine-enforced property rather than an inferred or assumed one.


Key safety-relevant properties include:

• Authority is validated through real-time presence and continuity rather than static credentials or historical permissions.

• Refusal is treated as a valid and expected system state, not an exception.

• Cached or stale permissions are explicitly invalidated.

• Execution is prevented under ambiguity rather than deferred to post-hoc review.

• When authority is unclear or contested, agency collapses instead of being redistributed to the system.


This distinction is central from a safety perspective: monitoring observes failure, whereas EchoAuth prevents unsafe execution by construction.


EchoAuth is designed for a specific high-risk context: autistic children who cannot reliably self-authorize, and the parents, caregivers, school staff, and clinical staff who are responsible for them. These environments make delegation drift particularly costly, as errors are not easily reversible and affected individuals may be unable to self-correct or advocate.


Importantly, EchoAuth is non-diagnostic and does not infer intent, behavioral state, or medical status. It does not attempt to resolve human disagreement or automate judgment. Its sole function is to enforce authorization constraints that ensure actions affecting a protected individual only occur when verified human authority is present, current, and coherent across settings such as home, school, and clinics.


From an EA perspective, EchoAuth represents a form of authorization-first safety infrastructure: a targeted, tractable intervention that reduces a known class of real-world AI failure without relying on stronger models, better alignment tuning, or sustained human monitoring. It aims to reduce harm not by making systems smarter, but by making unsafe actions structurally impossible.
