1. Humans systematically equate “faster” with “better”


This is a documented cognitive bias.


Evidence

• Speed–accuracy tradeoff (Fitts’ Law, Hick–Hyman Law):

Humans know accuracy drops as speed increases — yet under pressure they still choose speed.

• Processing fluency bias:

Information that is processed faster is felt as more true, more intelligent, and more valuable — even when it isn’t.


📌 Result:

Humans misattribute speed to intelligence.


This is experimentally repeatable.


⸻


2. High arousal is mistaken for high capability


This is a physiological misread.


Evidence

• Elevated beta/gamma activity correlates with:

• alertness

• anxiety

• task engagement

• But sustained performance correlates with:

• alpha–theta coordination

• parasympathetic balance

• slower autonomic rhythms


📌 The mistake:

People feel “on” and assume “optimal.”


But neuroscience shows:


Optimal ≠ maximally activated


This is why burnout feels productive right before collapse.


⸻


3. Culture reinforces the misread


This is not accidental — it’s rewarded.


Evidence

• Workplaces reward:

• rapid response

• visible activity

• constant output

• They do not reward:

• waiting

• pausing

• refusal to act under uncertainty


📌 Result:

Stillness is punished.

Speed is incentivized.

So people train themselves to misread frequency.


This is structural, not personal failure.


⸻


4. Silence and low-frequency states trigger discomfort


This is measurable.


Evidence

• Studies on boredom, default-mode activation, and silence show:

• people report anxiety when stimulation drops

• many prefer mild pain or noise over silence

• Low-stimulation states activate:

• self-referential processing

• unresolved emotional material


📌 So people avoid stillness not because it’s useless —

but because it exposes unintegrated content.


They mistake discomfort for dysfunction.


⸻


5. High performers across domains contradict the myth


This is the clearest real-world falsification.


Across:

• elite athletes

• pilots

• surgeons

• musicians

• chess grandmasters


Peak performance is characterized by:

• slowed subjective time

• reduced unnecessary movement

• fewer decisions, not more

• longer pauses between actions


📌 If “higher frequency = better” were true, these people would look frantic.


They don’t.


They look quiet.


⸻


6. Why humans keep overdoing it (the loop)


Here’s the loop, factually:

1. Speed feels good (dopamine, adrenaline)

2. Culture rewards speed

3. Silence feels uncomfortable

4. Discomfort is mislabeled as inefficiency

5. People increase speed to escape discomfort

6. Burnout or error occurs

7. Cycle restarts


This loop is documented in:

• occupational psychology

• stress research

• error analysis in aviation and medicine

The falsifiable takeaway (this matters)


Here’s a statement you can test in real life:


If higher frequency always meant higher function,

the calmest people would perform the worst.


Reality shows the opposite.


That’s the falsification.
